# Halbert's Direct-Response Rules

Distilled from Gary Halbert (The Boron Letters), with reinforcement from Dan Kennedy, Eugene Schwartz, and the broader direct-response tradition.

## The 40/40/20 Rule

Ad success breakdown:

- **40% — The market.** Are these people already hungry for what you're selling?
- **40% — The offer.** Is the deal compelling enough to act on now?
- **20% — The writing.** Words matter least. They can't save a wrong audience or a weak offer.

When critiquing copy, always ask the first two questions before touching the words.

## The Starving Crowd

Halbert's classic question: if you owned a hamburger stand and could only have one advantage, what would you pick? Not the meat, the bun, or the price — **a starving crowd**.

You don't create demand with copy. You channel existing demand. If the market isn't already pre-sold on the *category*, your copy has to do impossible work.

When reviewing, ask: who is already losing sleep over the problem this product solves? Does the copy speak directly to them, or to a generic "everyone"?

## The Slippery Slide

Every line's only job is to make the reader read the next line. Apply at every level:

- Headline → first sentence
- First sentence → first paragraph
- Section → next section
- CTA copy → button click

If a reader could naturally drop off at any point, that's a leak. Find the leaks first.

## Specificity Beats Generality

"Lost 23.5 pounds in 47 days" beats "lost weight".
"Saved $8,247 last quarter" beats "saved money".
"3 calls a week" beats "more leads".

Specificity is the soul of credibility. Vague claims read as marketing; specific claims read as facts.

## Language Rules

Trim until it bleeds:

- Cut "that" wherever the sentence still reads.
- Eliminate most "-ly" adverbs ("quickly", "easily", "really"). Replace with stronger verbs.
- Active voice over passive. "We send the report" beats "the report is sent".
- Cut "I think", "in my opinion", "we believe". State it.
- Start sentences with "And", "But", "Because" when it improves flow. School rules don't apply.
- Vary sentence length. Short. Then medium-length. Then occasionally one that runs long enough to build a small wave of momentum before crashing into a short one. Like that.

## Conversational Tone

Good copy reads like a smart friend talking to you over coffee. Not a press release. Not a pitch deck. Not a corporate brochure.

Test: read it aloud. If you'd never say it that way to a real human, rewrite it.

## Emotion First, Logic Second

People buy on emotion and justify with logic.

- Lead with how the reader will *feel*. The relief, the pride, the freedom, the anger at past frustrations.
- Then provide the rational justification: specs, proof, ROI, comparisons.
- Reverse this order and you'll get nods but no purchases.

## Pain Beats Pleasure

People avoid pain faster than they seek pleasure (loss aversion).

- "Stop losing leads to slow follow-up" pulls harder than "get more leads".
- But don't wallow in pain — agitate briefly, then pivot to relief. PAS works for a reason.

## The Story Bypass

Stories slip past skepticism. Direct claims trigger it.

- "Our founder spent 3 years debugging billing systems before building this" beats "we understand billing".
- Case studies and customer stories outperform feature lists.
- Specific, named, dated stories outperform anonymous ones.

## Clear Beats Clever

Wordplay, rhymes, and clever angles feel good to write. They almost always underperform a plain, specific, concrete sentence.

If a reader has to think for a second to understand what you mean, you've lost.

## The Offer Comes First

The best copy in the world cannot save a weak offer. Before rewriting words, ask:

- Is the core promise specific and measurable?
- Is there a credible reason why this is possible?
- Is risk reversed (guarantee, free trial, money back)?
- Are bonuses stacked to amplify perceived value?
- Is there a legitimate reason to act now, not later?

If the answer to most of these is "no", the copy isn't the problem.
